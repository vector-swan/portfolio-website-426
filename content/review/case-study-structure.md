# Case Study Structure — Standard Template
> This is the skeleton. Not every section is required for every project.
> The order can flex. The voice should always be Mia's.

---

## 1. HERO
**What it contains:**
- Product name
- One sentence: what is this and why does it matter
- Chips: role, years, team size or context

**The test:** Someone who has never heard of this company should understand in 10 seconds what they're about to read about.

---

## 2. CONTEXT (optional, brief)
**What it contains:**
- What the company/org does, in plain terms
- Where this product sits in the bigger picture
- Only include this if the reader genuinely needs it to understand the problem

**The test:** If you can skip this and the problem section still makes sense, skip it.

---

## 3. THE PROBLEM
**What it contains:**
- What was broken, missing, or not yet built
- Concrete — not "there was no framework" but "every team was building the same loading state from scratch"
- Can be one paragraph or a short list. Not both.

**The test:** A reader should feel the problem, not just understand it.

---

## 4. WHO I WAS DESIGNING FOR
**What it contains:**
- The actual humans: their role, their context, what they were trying to do
- For platform/system work: the two-layer model (builders AND end users)
- Skip the persona template. Just describe them.

**The test:** Does this make the rest of the decisions feel grounded in real people?

---

## 5. WHAT RESEARCH SHOWED
**What it contains:**
- What methods you used (be specific: moderated sessions, NPS data, agent interviews, etc.)
- What you found — not a list of methods but a list of findings
- Include actual quotes when you have them
- Be honest about what you didn't know yet

**The test:** Could someone verify this? Does it feel like evidence, not decoration?

---

## 6. THE DECISION (the most important section)
**What it contains:**
- The key design call, and the reasoning behind it
- Especially: where there was tension, pushback, or a harder path taken
- What you didn't do, and why
- This is where "I pushed back" or "I advocated for X" lives

**The test:** Would a senior designer understand what was actually hard about this? Does it show judgment, not just output?

---

## 7. WHAT I BUILT
**What it contains:**
- The actual work, with images
- Features/components with specific explanations — not just names
- Image captions should be descriptive, not just labels

**The test:** Can someone understand what each thing does without clicking anything?

---

## 8. SOMETHING THAT CHANGED HOW I WORK (optional but valuable)
**What it contains:**
- A moment in the process that shifted your approach
- A constraint that pushed you somewhere better
- Something you'd only know from doing this specific project

**The test:** Is this specific to this project, or could it have been said about any project? If it could be said about anything, cut it.

---

## 9. WHAT DIDN'T MAKE IT (when relevant)
**What it contains:**
- Features or directions that were validated but not shipped
- Your honest take on why
- What it tells you about the gap that still exists

**The test:** Does including this make you look thoughtful and honest, or does it just add length? Include it when it changes how the reader understands the project.

---

## 10. IMPACT
**What it contains:**
- Numbers when you have them (be specific about the time period)
- If no numbers: a concrete observable outcome
- Honest about what the numbers don't show

**The test:** Would this number mean something to someone who doesn't work at Workday?

---

## 11. WHAT I'D DO DIFFERENTLY (optional, strong when used)
**What it contains:**
- Genuine retrospective — what you'd change with hindsight
- Not self-flagellation. Just honest.
- This is the section from the old TRELORA portfolio that stood out most.

**The test:** Does this feel like something you'd actually say out loud, or does it feel performed?

---

## 12. CLOSING
**What it contains:**
- Either: a quote that captures something you genuinely believe about the work
- Or: what came next (how this project led to the next thing)
- Not both.

**The test:** Does it feel like an ending, or does it feel like filler?

---

## VOICE REMINDERS

From reading your old work — things to preserve:

- Use "I" when it was your decision. Use "we" when it was collaborative. Don't hedge.
- Be specific about research. Don't say "I did user interviews." Say what you learned from them.
- Honest retrospectives are more memorable than polished outcomes. Include them.
- Captions on images should describe what you're showing AND why it matters, not just name the screen.
- Short sentences for the things that matter. Long sentences for context.
- No aphorisms as section headers. (See: AI language cleanup pass already done.)

## WHAT EACH CURRENT CASE STUDY HAS / NEEDS

**GenAI Text Framework**
- Has: problem, two-audience framing, key decisions, production examples, cross-org impact, metrics
- Needs: stronger retrospective / what I'd do differently section (currently missing)
- Consider: the "What I Learned" section reads close but could be more personal and specific

**Prompt Engineering Studio**
- Has: research with quotes, the decision with pushback, honest advocacy section, metrics
- Needs: review for voice — read it out loud, flag anything that doesn't sound like you
- Strong: the Prompt Wizard section is the most "Mia" part of the whole portfolio

**Multi-Surface Agent Design**
- Needs: everything. Currently thin on research, decisions, and impact.
- Start with: what was the actual problem this work was solving?
